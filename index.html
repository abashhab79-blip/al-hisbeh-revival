01 — Context

The Market That Was
Hebron's Heartbeat

Al-Hisbeh (الحسبة) was once the most vibrant market and central meeting point in Hebron's old city. Farmers and agricultural workers travelled from across the governorate to sell their produce, while families, traders, and rural women came to buy seasonal vegetables.

More than a marketplace, it served as an open breathing space — rare in the densely packed urban fabric — and a daily hub connecting the old city to Hebron and its historic core.

Since its forced closure in 2000 by Israeli military order, the market has been seized, and its building announced for settlement expansion following October 2023. The surrounding souqs — Khan al-Shaheen and Souq al-Qazazin — struggle without their commercial anchor.

03 — Timeline

A History of Displacement

Pre-1948
Al-Hisbeh as Urban Artery
The Hisbeh functioned as the main produce market of Hebron's old city, connecting farmers from across the governorate to craftsmen, vendors, and the Ibrahimi Mosque. The market and adjacent souqs formed the commercial backbone of the city.
1948
Nakba — The Catastrophe
Mass displacement of Palestinian population. The 1951 Absentee Property Law prevented displaced Palestinians from returning to their properties, placing them under state control and opening them to privatization.
1967
Naksa — Israeli Occupation of West Bank
Israeli military occupation of the West Bank and Gaza Strip begins. Hebron, including the old city and Al-Hisbeh, comes under military administration. Settlement activity in and around the old city begins.
1993
Oslo Accords — Bantustanization
The Oslo Accords crystallize Palestinian territorial authority into disconnected enclaves — West Bank and Gaza only — marking the end of East Jerusalem as a Palestinian capital and beginning a new phase of urban displacement in Hebron.
1997
Hebron Protocol — Old City Division
Hebron is divided into H1 (Palestinian Authority) and H2 (Israeli military control). The old city falls under H2, severely restricting Palestinian movement and commerce.
2000
Al-Hisbeh Closure
Al-Hisbeh market is forcibly closed by Israeli military order. Over 200 shops in the surrounding area are shuttered. The market's closure devastates the commercial and social life of the old city.
2017–2018
Rehabilitation Committee Interventions
The Hebron Rehabilitation Committee rehabilitates Khan al-Shaheen market with UNESCO/SIDA funding, restores Hammam al-Na'im, and carries out infrastructure projects in adjacent neighborhoods. Adjacent housing units are restored and repopulated.
Post-2023
Escalation & Seizure
Following October 7, 2023, Israeli occupation authorities announce the seizure of the Hisbeh building and expansion of settlement outposts. Restrictions and closures intensify. Shop owners and residents demonstrate resilience by maintaining their presence.
Present
The Proposal — This Project
This project proposes reviving the spatial functionality of Al-Hisbeh as a weekly produce market at the back entrance of the Hisbeh site, between Khan al-Shaheen and Souq al-Qazazin, with a merchant hostel near Hammam al-Na'im.
04 — Proposal

Recreating the Spatial Functionality

The project proposes three interconnected interventions to revive Al-Hisbeh's role as the commercial and social artery of Hebron's old city — without waiting for the building itself to be unlocked.

01
Weekly Produce Market

A rotating weekly market at the back entrance of the Hisbeh site, in the passage between Khan al-Shaheen and the end of Souq al-Qazazin. Vendors from across the Hebron Governorate bring seasonal produce — recreating the market's role without occupying the seized building.

02
Merchants' Hostel

Adaptation of the old guest house near Hammam al-Na'im into overnight accommodation for travelling traders. This directly addresses the barrier preventing farmers from distant villages from participating in market days — restoring the Hisbeh's historic role as a regional hub.

03
Souq al-Qazazin Integration

Coordinated rehabilitation of Souq al-Qazazin to function in tandem with the market intervention, creating a continuous active zone from Khan al-Shaheen through the back entrance of the Hisbeh to the Ibrahimi Mosque — restoring the old city's historic commercial spine.

05 — Field Findings

Voices from the Old City

Interviews with residents, traders, and the Hebron Rehabilitation Committee reveal the depth of loss — and the resilience that persists.

How did people use the Hisbeh?
The Hisbeh was one of the most vibrant markets in the region. Farmers and agricultural workers came from across the governorate to sell produce. Beyond the building, the site resembled an open market full of goods and constant movement — vegetables, secondhand clothing (bale), wholesale and retail trade, frequented by families and rural women selling seasonal goods.
What social role did it play beyond commerce?
The Hisbeh served as a gathering place for residents of the old city, the people of Hebron, and surrounding villages — a daily meeting hub and a central point connecting the city to its historic core. It also served as a rare open breathing space, with courtyards for displaying goods in the dense urban fabric.
What forms of everyday resilience exist today?
Shop owners have demonstrated resilience by maintaining their presence in surrounding markets despite closures. The steadfastness of residents in the area remains evident. The Hebron Rehabilitation Committee continues to restore adjacent housing and markets, maintaining Palestinian presence and activity within the old city.
What rehabilitation work has already happened?
The Hebron Rehabilitation Committee has restored all adjacent housing units and repopulated them, carried out infrastructure rehabilitation, restored Hammam al-Na'im, and opened institutional offices nearby. Khan al-Shaheen market was fully rehabilitated in 2018 with UNESCO and SIDA funding.
How has the situation changed post-October 2023?
Conditions have significantly deteriorated, with increased restrictions and closures. Occupation authorities have announced the seizure of the Hisbeh building and expansion of additional settlement outposts, making the preservation of Palestinian commercial presence in the area more urgent than ever.
What is the main research objective?
How can we recreate the spatial functionality of the Hisbeh market within larger rehabilitation plans for Hebron's old city? The answer lies in operating within accessible zones — the passageways and adjacent souqs — rather than requiring recovery of the seized building itself.
